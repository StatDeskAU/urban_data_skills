# Digital Planning Competency Framework

The Planning Institute of Australia's **Digital Planning Core Competencies** framework provides a structured way to think about the skills urban planners need in an increasingly data-driven profession. This page maps the framework to practical learning pathways and self-assessment approaches.

The framework identifies four interconnected themes, each with **core** competencies (expected of all planners) and **specialist** competencies (for those in technical or senior roles).

---

## The Four Themes

### A. Data & Technology

The technical foundation — knowing how to work with data and the tools that process it.

**Core competencies:** Spreadsheet proficiency (pivot tables, data validation, VLOOKUP/INDEX MATCH), understanding data formats (CSV, JSON, GeoJSON, shapefiles), basic GIS skills, file management and version control.

**Specialist competencies:** Programming in Python or R, PostGIS and spatial databases, API development and consumption, machine learning basics, workflow automation, web scraping.

**Concrete example:** Instead of "ability to use spreadsheets," the framework should aim for specifics like "create pivot tables to analyse development application processing times by zone" or "use Power Query to merge datasets from multiple councils."

### B. Analysis & Interpretation

The analytical layer — making sense of data and drawing sound conclusions.

**Core competencies:** Data literacy, critical thinking about sources and biases, basic statistical interpretation, understanding sampling and aggregation, data visualisation fundamentals.

**Specialist competencies:** Spatial modelling and analysis (space syntax, network analysis), predictive analytics, data storytelling and narrative construction, time series analysis, reproducible research methods.

### C. Communication & Engagement

The human dimension — sharing insights and engaging communities through digital means.

**Core competencies:** Explaining technical outputs to elected officials and community members, basic digital engagement, understanding when digital tools are and aren't appropriate for community consultation.

**Specialist competencies:** Interactive dashboard development, web mapping and spatial visualisation, managing misinformation in digital engagement, bridging digital literacy gaps in communities, designing culturally appropriate digital engagement methods.

**Often overlooked:** The communication theme in many frameworks focuses heavily on tools (Tableau, Power BI) but underweights the human dimensions — explaining a complex spatial analysis to a council meeting, handling contentious digital engagement, or knowing when a face-to-face conversation is more appropriate than a dashboard.

### D. Governance & Ethics

The professional responsibility layer — ensuring data work serves planning values.

**Core competencies:** Data privacy and security, responsible AI use, documenting methodology and decisions for transparency, understanding data licensing and intellectual property.

**Specialist competencies:** Ethical decision-making frameworks (including flowcharts for when to use/not use AI), Indigenous data sovereignty and the CARE principles, policy development for digital planning systems, vendor claim assessment, addressing power dynamics in digital planning.

## Skills by Career Stage

The competency expectations should differ by career stage and organisational context. A common gap in competency frameworks is the failure to clarify what's expected at each level.

### Graduate Planner (First 1–2 years)
**Focus:** Building foundations — data literacy, spreadsheet proficiency, basic GIS, understanding data sources and formats. Learning to critically assess data quality and identify potential biases.

**Suggested pathway:** Start with The Carpentries workshops or Coursera's introductory data courses. Practice with ABS census data and local council open data. Focus on understanding *what* data is available and *where* to find it before worrying about advanced tools.

### Mid-Career Planner (Years 3–7)
**Focus:** Developing technical depth — programming (Python or R), advanced GIS analysis, database queries, data visualisation for reports and community engagement. Beginning to link datasets across agencies.

**Suggested pathway:** DataCamp or Codecademy for Python fundamentals. ESRI Academy or QGIS tutorials for advanced spatial work. Start a project-based learning approach — pick a real planning problem and solve it with data.

### Senior Planner / Specialist (Years 7+)
**Focus:** Strategic capability — statistical modelling, automation, API development, team capability building, governance frameworks. Connecting data work to planning values and organisational strategy.

**Suggested pathway:** University-level statistics or machine learning courses. Focus on reproducible research and documentation. Mentor junior staff and build team capability rather than concentrating all skills in one person.

### Manager (Any level)
**Focus:** Organisational capability — digital maturity assessment, skills audits across teams, advocating for digital investment, avoiding key-person dependency. Understanding enough about data work to make good resourcing decisions without needing to do the work personally.

**Suggested pathway:** Review the PIA competency framework with your team. Conduct a simple skills audit using the self-assessment approach below. Build a team development plan that spreads skills across multiple people.

## Context Matters: Different Planners, Different Paths

Competency development isn't one-size-fits-all. The framework is more useful when applied to specific contexts:

### Sam — Regional Council, Limited Resources
A planner in a small regional council with a tight budget and no dedicated data team. Sam needs practical, low-cost tools that deliver immediate value — QGIS rather than ArcGIS, free online courses rather than expensive training, spreadsheet-based analysis before investing in programming skills. The key challenge is doing more with less while maintaining data quality.

### Jordan — State Government Policy Planner
Working with large datasets across multiple agencies, Jordan needs skills in cross-agency data linkage, database management, and statistical analysis. The challenge is navigating data silos across different government departments and dealing with datasets that use different geographies, time periods, and standards.

### Gem — Private Consultancy
In a consultancy environment, Gem needs to build client-facing dashboards, tell compelling data stories, and work efficiently across multiple projects. Commercial tools (Tableau, ArcGIS) are available, but time pressure is constant. Skills in automation and template-based workflows are particularly valuable.

### Kai — Indigenous Community Engagement
Working with Indigenous communities requires culturally appropriate digital engagement methods. Kai needs to understand the CARE principles for Indigenous data governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) and know when digital tools are not appropriate. This persona is often missing from technology-focused competency frameworks but is critical for Australian planning practice.

## Self-Assessment Approach

A simple self-assessment can help planners identify their development priorities. For each competency area, rate yourself on a 1–5 scale:

| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 1 | No exposure — haven't encountered this |
| 2 | Awareness — understand the concept but can't apply it |
| 3 | Basic application — can do simple tasks with guidance |
| 4 | Proficient — can work independently |
| 5 | Expert — can teach others and handle complex scenarios |

**Sample self-assessment questions:**

- Can I create pivot tables to analyse urban data? (Theme A)
- Can I critically assess the quality and limitations of a dataset? (Theme B)
- Can I explain a spatial analysis to a non-technical audience? (Theme C)
- Do I know when to use — and when not to use — AI tools in my work? (Theme D)
- Can I link datasets from different agencies using common geographic identifiers? (Theme A/B)
- Do I understand the FAIR and CARE principles for data governance? (Theme D)

**Using the results:** Focus development on areas rated 1–2. Areas rated 3 benefit most from project-based practice. Areas rated 4–5 are opportunities to mentor others and build team capability.

## Connecting Skills to Planning Values

Digital competencies are sometimes presented as purely technical skills, disconnected from the values that underpin planning as a profession. This is a missed opportunity. Every data skill should connect back to planning's core purpose.

### Equity
**Question to ask:** Who benefits from this data system? Who is excluded?

**Relevant skills:** Disaggregating data by demographics, accessibility auditing of digital engagement tools, understanding how algorithmic decisions can embed or amplify bias, ensuring digital consultation reaches diverse communities.

### Sustainability
**Question to ask:** Does this analysis account for long-term environmental impacts?

**Relevant skills:** Climate data integration, scenario modelling, lifecycle thinking, environmental indicator tracking, remote sensing and satellite data analysis.

### Public Interest
**Question to ask:** Is this analysis transparent and explainable to the community?

**Relevant skills:** Data storytelling, open data publication, methodology documentation, making dashboards accessible and interpretable, using plain language to explain technical findings.

### Professional Judgment
**Question to ask:** Am I critically evaluating this output, or just trusting the algorithm?

**Relevant skills:** AI literacy and responsible use, vendor claim assessment, understanding model limitations, knowing when technology is not the right solution, maintaining the role of human judgment in planning decisions.

## Barriers to Adoption

Building these competencies is not just about individual motivation — there are systemic barriers that organisations and the profession need to address.

**Organisational barriers:** Legacy systems and budget constraints, key-person dependency for technical skills, resistance to change, limited digital maturity in smaller councils, difficulty building a business case for digital investment.

**Individual barriers:** Unclear competency expectations across career stages, ambiguous threshold between "core" and "specialist" skills, limited access to training and mentoring, difficulty knowing where to start.

**Community barriers:** Digital literacy gaps in communities being consulted, managing misinformation in digital engagement, knowing when digital tools are not appropriate, power dynamics around who controls and benefits from digital planning systems.

**Addressing barriers:** Organisations should conduct regular skills audits, spread technical capability across teams rather than concentrating it in individuals, allocate time and budget for professional development, and adopt a "living document" approach to competency frameworks that keeps pace with technological change.
