# Presenting Statistics: Style, Standards, and Storytelling

Understanding data and questioning it are essential skills — but they're only valuable if you can **communicate your findings clearly** to the people who need to act on them. A council report, a community consultation document, a policy brief, or a dashboard must present statistics in ways that are accurate, accessible, and honest.

This page covers the practical conventions for presenting numbers, tables, and charts in Australian government and planning contexts, drawing on authoritative style guides and professional best practice.

*For understanding data, see [Data Literacy](../data-literacy). For questioning data, see [Critical Thinking](../critical-thinking).*

### ONS Service Manual (UK Office for National Statistics)

[service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation) — the most comprehensive public guide to presenting data in official publications. While UK-focused, the principles apply universally:

- [Guidance — Principles](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation/guidance/principles) — when and how to use charts
- [Chart types](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation/chart-types) — when to use each chart type
- [Chart text](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation/guidance/chart-text) — titles, subtitles, footnotes, sources
- [Colour guidance](https://service-manual.ons.gov.uk/data-visualisation/colour) — colour palettes and accessibility

## Writing Numbers

The Australian Government Style Manual provides the definitive rules for numbers in government content. The key conventions for planning documents:

### When to use numerals vs words

Use **numerals** for most numbers: "There are 7 sheets of paper left." "The plan includes 4 new wind farms." Numerals are generally easier for people to scan than words, particularly in data-rich content.

Write **zero** and **one** in words: "Only one person applied." "The government has set a target of net-zero emissions." This avoids confusion with the letter O and the letter l in some typefaces.

Use **words** for a number that starts a sentence: "Eighty-nine people voted." Better yet, restructure to avoid starting with a number: "A total of 89 people voted."

### Commas and spacing

Use commas in numbers with 4 or more digits: 1,000 … 23,291 … 1,456,789. Separate digits into groups of three, working from right to left.

### Percentages

Use the % symbol with a numeral: "A 1% levy funded the remediation." "That work is 95% complete."

In running text, consider whether to use percentage points or percentages when describing changes. "Unemployment rose from 4% to 6%" is a 2-percentage-point increase but a 50% increase. These are very different claims, and confusing them is a common error in planning reports.

### Ranges

Use "to" rather than a dash for number ranges in running text: "3,000 to 25,000 people." Use an en-dash in tables and compact formats: "3,000–25,000."

### Decimal places

Be consistent within a document and match precision to what the data supports. Don't report a figure to 4 decimal places if the underlying data was collected to the nearest whole number. As a general rule, one decimal place is usually sufficient for percentages (e.g., "14.3%") and whole numbers are appropriate for population counts.

### Rounding

Round consistently and state your rounding convention. "Figures rounded to the nearest hundred" or "Percentages may not sum to 100 due to rounding" should appear as footnotes where relevant. Unexplained rounding errors (a table where column percentages add to 99% or 101%) undermine reader confidence.

## Presenting Tables

Tables are the workhorse of data presentation in planning documents — development application summaries, demographic profiles, housing supply tables, and infrastructure costings all rely on well-structured tables.

### When to use a table

Use a table when you need to present **exact values** that readers will want to compare or reference. If you only have one or two data points, report them in running text instead. If you want to show a trend or pattern, a chart may be more effective than a table.

### Structure and alignment

The Australian Government Style Manual is clear on alignment:

- **Left-align text** (suburb names, category labels, descriptions)
- **Right-align numbers** so that digits and decimal points line up vertically, making it easy to scan and compare values down a column
- **Align column headings** with the content below them — left-aligned headings for text columns, right-aligned headings for number columns
- Include the **unit** in the column heading (e.g., "Population (persons)" or "Area (ha)") rather than repeating it in every cell

### Headings and labels

Make tables **self-explanatory** — some readers will look at the table before reading the surrounding text. Every table should have:

- A **title** that describes the content: "Table 3: Development applications determined by type, City of Stirling, 2020–2024"
- **Clear column and row headings** using simple language
- A **source line** identifying where the data came from
- **Footnotes** for any caveats, definitional notes, or suppression indicators

### Common table mistakes in planning documents

**Merged cells:** These cause significant accessibility problems for screen readers and make tables harder to copy and reuse. Avoid them wherever possible.

**Too many columns:** Wide tables are hard to read, especially on screen and on mobile devices. If your table has more than 7 or 8 columns, consider whether it could be split into two tables or restructured.

**Inconsistent units:** Mixing thousands and millions in the same column, or switching between hectares and square metres, creates confusion. Pick one unit per column and stick to it.

**Missing totals:** If a column should sum to a total (or to 100%), include the total row. If it doesn't sum due to rounding, say so.

**Unsorted data:** Tables should be ordered meaningfully — alphabetically, by size, chronologically, or by whatever ordering helps the reader find what they need. Arbitrary ordering makes data harder to use.

## Presenting Charts and Visualisations

The ONS Service Manual provides the most comprehensive guidance on chart design for official statistics. The core principles apply equally to Australian planning documents.

### Fundamental principles

**Use charts to show the main findings, not to decorate.** Every chart should have a clear purpose — showing a trend, comparison, or pattern that supports the text. If a chart doesn't add understanding beyond what the text already says, remove it.

**Charts must work alongside text, not replace it.** The ONS guidance emphasises that charts should be placed close to the text they relate to, and the surrounding text should provide additional context or interpretation. A chart without commentary is an invitation for the reader to draw their own (possibly wrong) conclusions.

**Choose the right chart type for the data and the story.** A bar chart for comparisons between categories, a line chart for trends over time, a map for spatial patterns. The ONS chart types guidance provides detailed advice on when to use each type.

### Chart text and annotation

Every chart needs, at minimum:

- A **title** that describes the main finding or trend — not just the topic. "Dwelling approvals fell sharply in outer suburbs during 2023" is more useful than "Dwelling approvals by region." The ONS recommends that chart titles describe the trend, helping readers understand and remember the key point.
- A **subtitle** describing the data: measure, geography, time period. "Number of dwelling approvals, Greater Perth SA3s, January 2020 to December 2024."
- **Axis labels** with units. Don't make readers guess what the y-axis represents.
- A **source line** crediting the data: "Source: ABS, Building Approvals, Australia (cat. 8731.0)."
- **Footnotes** for caveats, methodology notes, or data quality warnings — but use sparingly. Too many footnotes clutter the chart.

### Common chart mistakes

**Truncated y-axes:** Starting a bar chart's y-axis at a value other than zero exaggerates differences. A bar chart showing values of 98, 99, 100, and 101 with a y-axis starting at 97 makes tiny differences look dramatic. Line charts have more flexibility — starting above zero can be appropriate to show meaningful variation — but the choice should be deliberate and flagged.

**Too many categories or series:** A line chart with 15 overlapping lines is unreadable. If you have many categories, consider using small multiples (the same chart repeated for each category) or highlighting only the most important series.

**3D charts, pie chart overuse, and chartjunk:** Three-dimensional effects distort perception. Pie charts are hard to read accurately (especially with more than 4-5 segments) and should generally be replaced with bar charts. Decorative elements that don't encode data (gradient fills, background images, shadow effects) reduce clarity.

**Misleading colour choices:** Colour should encode information, not decorate. Use sequential colour scales for continuous data (e.g., population density) and categorical colours for discrete groups. Ensure sufficient contrast for accessibility — approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of colour vision deficiency. The ONS colour guidance provides tested palettes that work for most users.

**Dual axes:** Charts with two different y-axes (e.g., population on the left, percentage on the right) are almost always misleading because the visual relationship between the two series depends entirely on how the axes are scaled. Avoid them.

## Writing About Statistics

The hardest part of presenting statistics isn't the formatting — it's writing about numbers in a way that is accurate, accessible, and not misleading.

### Be precise about what you're reporting

Say "median household income" not "the income." Say "at SA2 level" not "by suburb." Say "as at June 2021" not "currently." Say "based on Census self-reporting" not "the actual figure."

Imprecise language around statistics is one of the most common problems in planning documents. A sentence like "the population of Joondalup is 170,000" could mean the resident population, the daytime service population, the Census count, or the estimated resident population — and the differences can be substantial.

### Contextualise numbers

Raw numbers rarely mean anything without context. "450 development applications were lodged" is a fact. "450 development applications were lodged, a 23% increase on the previous year and the highest volume since 2018" is information. "450 development applications were lodged — a 23% increase driven primarily by a surge in grouped dwelling applications following the R-Codes amendment — putting significant pressure on the assessment team's capacity" is insight.

### Acknowledge uncertainty

Not all numbers are equally reliable. Population estimates have confidence intervals. Survey results have sampling error. Projections have assumptions. Geocoded addresses have spatial accuracy limits.

Where precision is not warranted, say so: "approximately 15,000" rather than "15,000." Where data has known limitations, flag them: "Based on Census data, which may undercount short-term residents." Where projections are involved, distinguish scenarios from forecasts.

### Avoid common statistical language traps

**"Up to"** — "Up to 500 new dwellings" could mean any number from zero to 500. It's technically accurate but functionally meaningless. Prefer a range or a specific estimate with caveats.

**"Significant"** — In everyday language this means "important." In statistics it means "unlikely to have occurred by chance." Using "significant" in a planning report without specifying which meaning you intend creates ambiguity. Consider "substantial" or "notable" for the everyday meaning, and "statistically significant" (with a p-value or confidence level) for the technical meaning.

**"Average"** — This could mean mean, median, or mode. In planning contexts involving prices, incomes, or processing times, the mean and median often differ substantially because the data is skewed. Always specify which average you're using.

**"Per capita" and "per household"** — These give different results for the same underlying data because household sizes vary. "Per capita" income is always lower than "per household" income, and the gap varies by area. Make sure you're comparing like with like.

## Accessibility

Presenting statistics accessibly isn't optional — it's a legal requirement under the *Disability Discrimination Act 1992* and a professional obligation.

### Tables
- Use proper table markup (header cells, data cells) — don't just format text to look like a table
- Avoid merged cells
- Include a table summary or caption for screen reader users
- Don't rely on colour alone to convey meaning

### Charts
- Provide a text alternative that describes the main finding
- Use sufficient colour contrast (check with accessibility tools)
- Don't rely on colour alone to distinguish data series — use patterns, labels, or different line styles as well
- Ensure interactive charts are keyboard-navigable

### Documents
- Use heading styles (not just bold text) so screen readers can navigate
- Include alt text for all images and charts
- Check colour contrast ratios (4.5:1 minimum for normal text)
- Test with a screen reader if possible

The Australian Government Style Manual's [accessibility guidance](https://www.stylemanual.gov.au/accessible-and-inclusive-content) provides comprehensive requirements.

## Quick Reference: Presenting Data in Planning Documents

| Element | Good Practice | Common Mistake |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Numbers in text | "7 new lots" / "one application" / "zero dwellings" | "seven new lots" (harder to scan) |
| Large numbers | 23,291 (with commas) | 23291 (no separators) |
| Percentages | "rose by 2 percentage points to 6%" | "rose by 2% to 6%" (ambiguous) |
| Table numbers | Right-aligned, decimal-aligned | Left-aligned (hard to compare) |
| Chart titles | Describe the finding: "Approvals fell in 2023" | Describe the topic: "Approvals by year" |
| Chart axes | Labelled with units, starting from zero for bar charts | Unlabelled, truncated, or dual axes |
| Sources | "Source: ABS, Cat. 8731.0, June 2024" | No source, or vague "ABS data" |
| Uncertainty | "approximately 15,000" or "estimated at 15,000 ± 500" | Presenting estimates as exact figures |
| Context | "450 DAs, up 23% on last year" | "450 DAs" (no benchmark) |
| Averages | "Median house price was $520,000" | "Average house price was $520,000" (which average?) |
